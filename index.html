Is your dream school worth the cost?

Prestige pressure is real — it shapes conversations, college lists, and family decisions. But when families actually make their choice, 63% say affordability mattered more than a school's reputation.

Is this school worth $20,000 more per year than the next option?

If you've felt the pressure to chase a brand-name college, you're not imagining it. And it's not unreasonable — those schools are genuinely excellent.

It starts at a dinner party, or in the comments on someone's Instagram post. Brand names circulate. Rankings get quoted. And slowly – without anyone saying it out loud – a hierarchy forms – and you start to wonder if you're aiming too low.

The question isn't whether those schools are good. It's whether they're the right fit for your student and your family's specific situation. And whether the premium is worth it.

Because when families actually make the decision, something interesting happens. Nearly two-thirds of parents say affordability matters more than reputation when choosing a college. In other words: prestige may dominate the conversation, but price usually decides the outcome.

Most families already sense the rankings race isn't the whole story. What they often lack isn't instinct – it's the research and confidence to push back when brand names take over the conversation.

What the evidence actually shows
JS
Jeff Selingo
Higher-ed researcher, author of Dream School

Higher-ed advisor Jeff Selingo surveyed more than 3,000 parents for his book Dream School and asked a simple question: What do you actually want for your child at the end of college? Two answers came back overwhelmingly:

A job. Parents want their child to launch into adulthood.
Connections. Relationships with peers, mentors, and faculty who help shape their future.

The good news: the research shows these outcomes are well within reach at a much wider range of schools than most families realize.

Research from the Opportunity Insights group at Harvard found that attending an "Ivy-Plus" college improves the odds of landing certain elite jobs or reaching the very top of the income distribution. And for some students, and some career paths, that matters. But much of that advantage reflects who gets admitted, not necessarily differences in educational quality. And the advantage applies most clearly to a narrow set of careers: Wall Street, elite consulting firms, and top law schools. Most students aren't headed there.

"

We tend to think about the outliers as the average. But the research shows that the typical outcomes of someone from Harvard aren't meaningfully different from graduates of Ohio State, UCLA, or many other public flagships.

Jeff Selingo  ·  Dream School

Selingo also analyzed data from the National Survey of Student Engagement, which collects responses from students at more than 900 colleges each year. Student satisfaction looked similar across schools. But one difference stood out:

"

At colleges accepting 20–40% of applicants, students report more interaction with faculty than at the most selective schools in the country.

Jeff Selingo  ·  Dream School

The most selective schools do have one consistent advantage: internships. It's a meaningful one, and worth factoring in. But that's just one factor on a much longer list of what shapes a student's experience and outcomes.

Financial fit isn't settling. It's a strategy.

Whether your student is aiming for a highly selective school or building a list from scratch, the same principle applies: find a school you can afford and that delivers the most within your budget.

Most families build their college list around three criteria: academic reach, comfort, and safety schools. Those categories have guided college planning for decades. But college counselor Shellee Howard of College Ready argues the framework is incomplete.

Financial fit shouldn't be a final category layered on at the end. It should shape the entire list from the start. That's why Howard introduces a fourth category many families overlook.

The myth Howard most wants families to abandon

"We'll figure out the money later — let's just focus on getting in."

This is how smart, loving families end up with $100,000 or more in loans and a student starting adult life in a financial hole. Getting in is not the finish line.

The Plan B school might actually be the better fit

In Dream School, Jeff Selingo tells the story of a student named William who applied early to a highly selective university, and got in. At first, it felt like winning the lottery.

But the excitement faded quickly once he arrived. The class he most wanted to take wouldn't be available until junior year. Undergraduate research opportunities were reserved for graduate students. And the campus culture felt relentlessly competitive. By winter break, William decided to transfer to a public research university in his home state.

William's story isn't an argument against ambitious schools. It's an argument for fit. Across dozens of student interviews, Selingo found the same pattern: students who land at schools where they're near the top of the applicant pool often gain access to things more selective schools can't offer — undergraduate research, smaller cohorts, study-abroad funding, and closer relationships with faculty.

"

One of the students I met started at a very small liberal arts college not even ranked in the top 100. She ended up at Harvard Law School and on the Harvard Law Review. The outcomes are there – even for students who start at colleges further down the rankings.

Jeff Selingo  ·  Dream School
